# MYTHS — popular but debunked or overclaimed teaching ideas

Deny-list guardrail against training-data priors. These are **not** low-tier techniques — they
have no "when to use" — which is why they live here, not in `catalog/`.

**Normative rule (see `TAXONOMY.md`)**: specs 002/003 MUST NOT cite any entry below as
justification for a course-design choice. Each entry carries both a popular-source citation
(provenance) and a debunking citation (evidence).

---

## 1. Learning styles (VAK/VARK and similar)

**Claim.** Learners have stable visual/auditory/kinesthetic (or similar) styles, and instruction
works best when matched to the preferred style. *(popular source: [P40])*

**Why it's popular.** Intuitive, matches self-perceived preferences, and heavily promoted in
teacher training and commercial inventories [P35].

**Why it's wrong.** The definitive review found no adequate evidence for the "meshing hypothesis"
— properly designed studies of style-matched instruction fail to show benefits [P35]; later
domain reviews (e.g. chemistry education) concur [P36].

**Use instead.** General cognitive principles for everyone — retrieval practice, spacing, dual
coding, clear explanations — offering multiple representations because the *material* benefits,
not because learners have style labels [P1][P12][P22].

**Citations.** Popular: [P40]. Debunking: [P35], [P36].

## 2. "Brain-based" neuromyths (left/right-brain learners, Brain Gym, …)

**Claim.** Learners are "left-brained" or "right-brained"; specific movement routines (Brain Gym)
boost cognition; oversimplified brain facts justify particular methods. *(popular source: [P41])*

**Why it's popular.** Neuroscience language carries prestige; simple brain claims promise easy
fixes and spread through workshops and commercial programs [P37].

**Why it's wrong.** The Nature Reviews Neuroscience survey of neuromyths found these beliefs
highly prevalent among teachers and scientifically unsupported — most misinterpret basic
neuroscience (both hemispheres participate in most tasks) or were never tested for classroom
impact [P37].

**Use instead.** Empirically grounded cognitive principles (spacing, retrieval, feedback), plus
only those educational-neuroscience findings tied to robust behavioral outcomes
[P33][P1][P12].

**Citations.** Popular: [P41]. Debunking: [P37].

## 3. Growth mindset as a big lever on achievement

**Claim.** Teaching students that intelligence is malleable produces large, reliable achievement
gains across contexts. *(popular source: [P42])*

**Why it's popular.** Motivationally appealing, easy to communicate, heavily promoted through
books, talks, and school programs [P42].

**Why it's overclaimed.** Two meta-analyses found mindset-achievement correlations are small and
intervention effects trivial-to-small, with meaningful benefits only in some at-risk subgroups;
mindset interventions alone are unlikely to move grades [P38]. The nuance: this is an
*overclaim*, not a fabrication — the debunked part is "big lever," not "mindset exists."

**Use instead.** Structural instruction improvements — effective practice, feedback, scaffolding
— with mindset messaging at most a modest adjunct [P30][P1][P12].

**Citations.** Popular: [P42]. Debunking: [P38].

---

## 4. Grit as a uniquely powerful predictor of success

**Claim.** Grit — perseverance and passion for long-term goals — is a distinct trait that
strongly predicts achievement beyond established factors. *(popular source: [P43])*

**Why it's popular.** Resonates with cultural values about persistence; the original study and
best-selling book framed it as a major determinant of success [P43].

**Why it's overclaimed.** An 88-study meta-analysis found grit's correlation with performance is
modest (r ≈ .18) and largely redundant with conscientiousness; grit's distinctiveness as a
construct is questionable and grit-only interventions likely misguided [P39].

**Use instead.** Supportive instructional design (effective practice, feedback, manageable
cognitive load) and broader self-regulation skills; persistence matters but doesn't substitute
for good learning conditions [P33][P6][P12].

**Citations.** Popular: [P43]. Debunking: [P39].
