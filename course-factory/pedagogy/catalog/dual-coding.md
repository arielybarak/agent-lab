# Dual coding (visual + verbal)

## What it is

Presenting information through both verbal (spoken or written) and non-verbal visual
representations (diagrams, images, animations) that are meaningfully aligned, so learners build
linked verbal and imagery codes. The theory posits two partially independent processing systems
whose coordinated activation enhances memory and comprehension [P22][P23].

## Evidence tier

**High for well-designed visual-verbal combinations — with the caveat that the gains vanish when
multimedia design principles are violated (see boundary conditions).** Paivio's dual-coding theory
and subsequent education reviews show words + relevant images beat words alone, especially for
concrete concepts [P22][P23]. Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning and associated
experiments support the conditions — coherence, contiguity, signaling — under which the
combination yields real gains [P24][P25][P28].

## When to use

- Visuals directly relevant (never decorative), aligned in time and space with the words —
  labeled diagrams synchronized with narration [P24][P25].
- Complex spatial, causal, or procedural content: science diagrams, system architectures,
  timelines; strongest for learners with lower prior knowledge [P24].
- Signal the key parts (highlighting) and strip extraneous detail (coherence) [P25].

## Boundary conditions & pitfalls

- **Redundancy effect**: wordy on-screen text + identical narration + a busy diagram increases
  extraneous load and impairs learning [P26][P27].
- **Decorative images distract**, particularly low-prior-knowledge learners, crowding out
  processing of the core idea [P24][P25].
- **Split attention**: explanation physically or temporally far from the diagram it explains
  forces the learner to integrate across a gap, erasing the benefit [P25][P26].

## Worked example

A neural-networks module presents a simple network diagram (input, hidden, output layers) beside
a concise textual explanation that references the diagram's labels directly, with each label
placed next to its component rather than in a separate legend. Where the toolchain supports it,
the forward-propagation walkthrough upgrades to an animated version that highlights the active
nodes in sync with the narration.

## Applying to narrative/history-heavy material

The high-payoff visuals are **timelines, maps, and causal chains** — structures narrative prose
hides. A history lesson pairs each period's text with an accumulating timeline/map that the prose
references directly ("the 1848 wave, third marker"). Portraits and scene paintings are decoration
— the failure mode above — unless the lesson's claim is *about* them.
