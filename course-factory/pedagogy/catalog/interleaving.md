# Interleaving

## What it is

Mixing different topics, problem types, or skills within a single practice session, rather than
blocking all examples of one type together. Interleaving creates contrasts between categories and
forces learners to repeatedly identify *which* strategy applies — supporting inductive learning
and discrimination [P16][P14].

## Evidence tier

**High for inductive/discriminative learning; medium overall; ambiguous for expository text.**
A multilevel meta-analysis (59 studies, 238 effect sizes) found a moderate overall effect
(Hedges g ≈ 0.42), strongest for visual materials, smaller but positive in mathematics, and
nonsignificant-to-negative for expository texts [P16]. Interleaving is theoretically distinct
from spacing — its mechanism is discriminative contrast, not distributed practice [P17]. In math,
interleaved practice can roughly double delayed test scores despite *worse* performance during
practice [P14].

## When to use

- When learners must choose between similar, confusable strategies or categories (problem types
  in math or physics, visual styles, classification tasks) [P16][P14].
- Inductive category learning where categories are similar to each other but items within a
  category vary [P16].
- Optimizing for delayed retention and transfer, once learners have initial familiarity with each
  individual type [P14][P16]; interleaving dimensions of a task also matters — interleave what
  must be discriminated [P18].

## Boundary conditions & pitfalls

- **Feels worse than it is**: practice performance drops, and unwarned learners read the mix as
  confusing or discouraging — tell them why the set is mixed [P14].
- **No discrimination demand, no benefit**: simple word lists and expository texts show no
  advantage, sometimes inferiority to blocking [P16].
- **Novice overload**: heavy interleaving of complex topics before baseline schemas exist
  interacts badly with cognitive load [P17][P6].

## Worked example

A calculus course generates practice sets where derivative, integral, and limit problems are
mixed within each session, so the learner must first classify the problem type, then apply the
technique. Early units interleave mildly (pairs of types); later units raise the mixture
complexity once each type is competent in isolation.

## Applying to narrative/history-heavy material

Mostly **don't** — expository/narrative text is where the meta-analytic evidence is ambiguous or
negative [P16]. Interleave only where a genuine discrimination target exists (e.g. distinguishing
historiographical schools when interpreting a document); otherwise keep narrative material
blocked and get durability from spacing + retrieval instead.
