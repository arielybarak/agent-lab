# Spaced repetition (distributed practice)

## What it is

Scheduling multiple study or practice sessions for the same material with time gaps between them,
instead of massing repetitions in a single session. Spacing — especially with some forgetting in
between — makes each retrieval more effortful, which strengthens long-term retention [P12].

## Evidence tier

**High.** A meta-analysis of distributed practice in verbal recall (839 effects across 317
experiments) shows strong, reliable spacing effects, with the optimal gap depending on the
desired retention interval [P12]. The gap effect is non-monotonic — a "temporal ridgeline" of
optimal spacing [P13]. Spacing replicates across vocabulary, conceptual learning, and procedural
skills [P12][P14], and a meta-analytic review confirms the benefit specifically for spacing out
retrieval-practice episodes [P15].

## When to use

- When the goal is durable learning over weeks or months, not short-term cramming [P12].
- Combined with retrieval practice at each review — spaced *testing* beats spaced re-reading
  [P12][P15].
- Both declarative (facts, concepts) and procedural skills, including language and math
  [P12][P14].
- Learners prefer massed practice despite worse long-term outcomes — the schedule must be built
  into the course, not left to choice [P12].

## Boundary conditions & pitfalls

- **Too-short gaps** approximate massed practice and shrink the benefit; **too-long gaps** cause
  complete forgetting and costly relearning [P12].
- **Passive reviews**: spacing still helps with re-reading, but gains are much smaller than with
  spaced retrieval [P12][P15].
- **Opaque schedules**: spacing plans the learner can't understand or trust reduce adherence and
  real-world impact [P12].

## Worked example

A statistics course teaches mean, variance, and confidence intervals, then schedules short
retrieval quizzes on prior units 2 days, 1 week, and 3 weeks after initial exposure. The
scheduler stretches the gap for content the learner previously retrieved successfully and
shortens it after failures, aiming for desirable difficulty at each review.

## Applying to code-heavy material

Spacing rarely means flashcards here; it means **structured reuse**: later lessons deliberately
re-invoke earlier constructs (the parser from lesson 2 becomes the input stage of lesson 5's
exercise), forcing re-retrieval of prior APIs and patterns inside new work. The syllabus is the
spacing schedule — plan the reuse chain when composing it, not per-lesson.
