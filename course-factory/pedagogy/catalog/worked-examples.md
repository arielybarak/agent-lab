# Worked examples

## What it is

Fully solved problems or tasks with explicit step-by-step solutions and rationales that learners
study before (or instead of) solving similar problems on their own. They expose the structure of
expert problem solving, cut unnecessary search, and help novices build schemas for complex tasks
[P6][P7]. The origin finding is the classic algebra study of Sweller & Cooper [P5].

## Evidence tier

**High.** Cognitive Load Theory research has repeatedly demonstrated the worked-example effect:
novices learn more efficiently from worked examples than from unguided problem solving,
particularly in mathematics and physics [P6][P7]. Replacing some conventional practice problems
with worked examples improves performance and reduces cognitive load [P8][P9][P10][P11].

## When to use

- Novice or intermediate learners meeting new, complex procedures (algebraic manipulation,
  programming patterns, multi-step problem solving) [P7].
- High element-interactivity domains, where unguided problem solving would overload working
  memory [P6][P7].
- Best when examples are segmented, highlight key steps, and are followed by a few carefully
  chosen practice problems that force active processing [P7][P10].

## Boundary conditions & pitfalls

- **Expertise reversal**: as learners gain skill, detailed worked examples become redundant, add
  extraneous load, and can hinder deeper problem solving — fade them out [P6][P7][P19].
- **Passive skimming**: without self-explanation prompts or a transition to independent practice,
  learners copy surface steps and fail to transfer [P7].
- **Bad examples backfire**: steps without the *why*, or irrelevant detail mixed in, overload
  memory and erase the effect [P7][P10].

## Worked example

An introductory data-science course generates a worked example of fitting and interpreting a
logistic regression: loading data, selecting features, fitting, interpreting coefficients,
evaluating with ROC curves. Each step carries a brief rationale ("logistic regression because the
outcome is binary") and an embedded prompt ("what would happen if we included feature X?"),
followed by a small set of similar problems where learners fill in missing steps
(completion problems), then solve fully on their own.

## Applying to narrative/history-heavy material

The "problem" is interpretive, not procedural: a worked example is a model analysis — a short
essay or argument annotated step by step (claim → evidence chosen → counter-evidence weighed →
conclusion), followed by a partially completed analysis of a parallel case for the learner to
finish. The fading logic is identical; only the artifact changes.
